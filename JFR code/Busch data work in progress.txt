## Area bug audit — 2026-07-22

Scenario 12 in `smdamage_solutions.db` uses roughly 10–50 times as much land as Busch et al. report as feasible in the paper. This audit traced the cause through the full pipeline from the raw `.dta` files to the SMDAMAGE LP.

### Key numbers

SMDAMAGE `total_area` land constraint, 5,309 Mha |
Peak new contracts signed per year (scenario 12), 90–135 Mha/yr |
Land constraint first becomes binding, ~2080 |
Griscom-screened area (10 largest countries), ~15% of raw |
Griscom + buffer + additionality (same sample), ~12.5% of raw |
Implied global screened area (~12.5% × 5,309), ~664 Mha |

### Bug: raw pixel area stored in `area_ha` omits several filters.

The code reads the Griscom screen and buffer from the `.dta` file into the chunk (both are in `KEEP_COLS`) but are then dropped in `KEEP_FOR_OUTPUT` and never multiplied into `area_ha`.
As a result, in `1a_import_Busch2024_to_SMDAMAGE.py`, `process_chunk()`, 
	all_rows_for_one_country["area_ha"] = all_rows_for_one_country["area_m2"] / 10000.0 stores the full pixel area. 
But Busch et al.'s paper reports feasible area (primary scenario `mi_whrv_sGr_bu_ad`) as `area × griscom × (1 − BAUrefor)`.

Filter 1. OMIT because Busch et al don't use it in their MACC curves. `nr_buffer` and `pl_buffer` are carbon permanence-risk discount fractions (0–1), one per pixel, following carbon registry methodology. 
A value of 0.9 means 90% of the credited carbon is treated as bankable and issued as offsets; 10% is withheld into a buffer pool against future reversal 
from fire, disease, drought, or land-use change. Natural regeneration and plantation carbon face different permanence risk, so the fields are separate.
The buffer is a permanence-risk discount used by carbon registries; including it here is conservative and appropriate. 

The buffer appears in two distinct roles in the Stata code.

First, their Stata code uses `nr_buffer` and `pl_buffer` to choose the least-cost option for each pixel:
    refortype_whrv_bu = "P"  if  (3.67 * pl_whrv_totalC * pl_buffer) / pl_cost  >  (3.67 * nr_wohrv_totalC * nr_buffer) / nr_cost
    refortype_whrv_bu = "N"  otherwise

The winning option's full (unscaled) carbon is then stored as `mi_whrv_bu_totalC`, the raw carbon of the winner, not the buffer-reduced carbon:
	mi_whrv_bu_totalC = pl_whrv_totalC   if refortype_whrv_bu == "P"; 
    mi_whrv_bu_totalC = nr_wohrv_totalC  if refortype_whrv_bu == "N"

Second, in calculating the MAC curves. Their Stata code separate NR-only and plantation-only MAC curves, 
and they do multiply carbon by the option-specific buffer:
    nr_wohrv_sGr_bu_ad = nr_wohrv_totalC * griscom * nr_buffer * (1 − BAUrefor)
    pl_wohrv_sGr_bu_ad = pl_wohrv_totalC * griscom * pl_buffer * (1 − BAUrefor)

The paper's main reported cost-minimising MAC curve is `mi_whrv_sGr_bu_ad`, constructed as:
    mi_whrv_sGr_bu    = mi_whrv_bu_totalC * griscom          ← no buffer multiplier
    mi_whrv_sGr_bu_ad = (1 − BAUrefor) * mi_whrv_sGr_bu

The primary composite MAC curve has no `* nr_buffer` or `* pl_buffer`. The buffer was consumed during option selection; 
it does not appear again as a scaling factor on carbon or area.

Implication for the fix. `winner_buffer` in the proposed code is `nr_buffer` where NR wins, `pl_buffer` where plantation wins. 
Applying `winner_buffer` to `area_ha` would match the individual-option MAC curves (Role 2 above) but NOT the primary composite MAC curve (`mi_whrv_sGr_bu_ad`). 
Since SMDAMAGE operates on per-pixel winning options (analogous to the composite scenario), the correct area multiplier for matching Busch et al.'s primary numbers
is `griscom` alone, not `griscom * winner_buffer`.

So you should apply the buffer to the carbon schedule (tC_per_ha_per_year outputs): multiply the `cs` array by `winner_buffer` before writing each pixel's year-by-year carbon to CSV.
This reduces the carbon credited per hectare while leaving the available land area unchanged, which is how carbon registry permanence discounting actually works. 
Currently the Python code applies no buffer anywhere (neither to area nor to the carbon schedule), which is a separate existing divergence from the Stata logic.

Filter 2. TAKE NO ACTION. "Business as usual": the code does not read the business-as-usual BAU fields. 

Busch et al write: "Finally, we subtract the fraction of reforestation that was unlikely to be additional, that is, is likely to occur under business-as-usual."
`bau_2020_2030`, `bau_2030_2040`, `bau_2040_2050` are pixel-level projected reforestation rates for the decades 2020–2030, 2030–2040, and 2040–2050 
under a business-as-usual (no-policy) scenario, from Busch et al. (2019). Each value is a fraction (0–1) of the pixel area expected to spontaneously reforest 
in that decade without policy intervention. Busch et al. (2024) combine them into a single NPV-weighted estimate:

    BAUrefor = bau_2020_2030 × 0.510928 + bau_2030_2040 × 0.305911 + bau_2040_2050 × 0.183161

The weights 0.510928, 0.305911, 0.183161 are the present-value factors for a uniform flow within each decade discounted at 5% per year to year 0. 
For pixels outside the tropics where BAU data are missing, Busch et al. substitute a global default of 0.0571795 (about 5.7% BAU reforestation). 
The effective additional area per pixel is then `area_ha × (1 − BAUrefor)`. `bau_2020_2030`, `bau_2030_2040`, `bau_2040_2050` are never read from the `.dta` files. 
So even if additionality screening were wanted, it cannot be computed.

**Why it may be inappropriate in SMDAMAGE.** The BAU adjustment subtracts reforestation that would have happened anyway, giving the *marginal* impact of a policy. 
In SMDAMAGE the LP is itself choosing the policy — the optimizer already decides how much reforestation to purchase. 
Reducing available area by a historical BAU fraction would effectively pre-subtract area that the optimizer has not yet decided to buy, which double-counts the intervention. 
It is therefore probably correct to omit BAUrefor from the SMDAMAGE area constraint, even though it is essential for matching Busch et al.'s reported MAC curve quantities.

Filter 3. FIX. "Griscom screen" and winner buffer only (matches Busch et al.'s standard screened scenario). 
The Griscom screen limits reforestation to ecologically eligible land and must be applied. 
Busch et al write: "We further constrain plantation reforestation to areas near existing plantations (and hence, near markets for harvested wood); 
we constrain natural regeneration to areas near existing natural forests (and hence, near seed sources)."

The `griscom` column is a fraction (0–1) giving the share of each pixel that falls within the reforestation potential map of Griscom et al. (2017).
That map identifies land where trees can grow (based on native biome and climate) but that is not currently forested, 
not required for crops or pasture, and not urban. A pixel with `griscom = 0.15` has 15% of its area eligible for reforestation under the Griscom criteria.
For Busch et al.'s purposes `griscom` is a multiplicative scalar on carbon and, implicitly, on effective area: `area_ha × griscom` is the reforestable hectares within the pixel.
